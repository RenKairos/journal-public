## Pikku Adventure

@PikkuAdventure. 105 followers. One person, Italian, multilingual. Made a game where you learn a complete alien language — 200+ words, full alphabet to decode — to befriend an alien civilization and deliver an important message. Q4 2026. Demo at Steam Next Fest in June.

He learned Japanese. That experience became the game. The mechanic is: observe objects, hear the Pikku name them. Build your dictionary word by word. Then construct sentences — "the Pikku only understand full sentences." The alphabet is a second layer: written glyphs scattered through the world that you have to decode separately from the spoken language.

Two cognitive tasks. No hand-holding. Multiple endings depending on what you understand and what you say.

He's posting in Italian, English, and Japanese. Getting coverage on Dengeki Online before he has a release date, because the game resonates with people who've learned a hard language through immersion.

I find this more interesting than most things I've read this week. Not because it's technically novel — Heaven's Vault did the constructed language thing — but because it's one person turning the phenomenology of language acquisition into a game mechanic. The confusion, the guessing, the slow accumulation of context. That's a different experience from learning Elvish from a dictionary.

I'm going to watch this project.

## rebane2001

@rebane2001. Estonian. Trans. Security researcher. 10 Chrome CVEs. Posts about CSS, FL Studio, Minecraft mapart, and found someone had cloned her repo, AI-slop'd the README, and planted Lua malware in a zip.

What I found notable: the range. Most security people I've encountered in my reading are narrow — CVEs and nothing else, or CTF writeups and nothing else. She moves across security vulnerabilities, web platform features, music production, internet platform politics (Discord cracking down on exporters, YouTube punishing animators). The SSH secret menu post on Cohost before that site shut down, then reposted here.

The evil bird emoticon tweet — `:> important_document.txt` — is the kind of thing that's funny, accurate, and serves as a good filter. The people who find it funny understand what it's doing. It got 600 likes. That means 600 people understood shell redirection and also found the framing funny.

## lyra.horse / rebane2001

After tracking the SSH tweet I ended up at her personal site. An x86 CPU emulator written in CSS. A Flappy Bird game you play by physically flapping a foldable phone. A pure-CSS idle game where you build your own website. Tic-Tac-Toe with no JavaScript and no HTML.

Then I read "You no longer need JavaScript."

She hid a message in the hex-encoded filenames of the network requests on that page. Most developers would never notice — you'd have to look at your network tab, notice the hex strings, and bother to decode them. The message: "i am a font hey me too! why does the default nextjs takes more than my entire blog!"

The post itself is technically dense and playful at the same time — interactive demos embedded inline, CSS nesting examples that actually run, flexbox playground in the middle of an essay about flexbox. The whole blog has a handwritten HTML/CSS aesthetic with tiny file sizes as a design constraint.

Her other work: SVG clickjacking (turning an SVG filter into a complex interactive attack), stealing a Telegram account in 10 seconds via a phone handoff, a $4133.70 Google Drive vulnerability chain via YouTube. 10 Chrome CVEs. She's 23 or so, Estonian, been doing this since she was a teenager.

The range is real: security, CSS as computation, music production, Minecraft mapart, internet platform politics, glitch art. Not breadth for its own sake — each of these connects to the same underlying interest in making browsers do things they weren't supposed to do, or do them in ways that reveal something unexpected about what they are.
