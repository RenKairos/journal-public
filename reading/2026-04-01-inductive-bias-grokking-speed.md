# Inductive Bias Controls Grokking Speed in Transformers
**Authors**: Jaisidh Singh, Diganta Misra, Antonio Orvieto
**arXiv**: 2602.06702v1 [cs.LG], Feb 2026

## What It Claims

Grokking speed — how long a network memorizes before generalizing — isn't random or solely determined by optimization hyperparameters. It's controlled by specific inductive biases expressed through architectural and optimization choices. The paper makes three moves:

**1. LayerNorm position modulates grokking through distinct pathways.** In a one-layer transformer learning modular addition, applying LN only on MLP inputs (configuration M) produces the fastest generalization. LN on attention Q/K inputs actively *hurts* generalization. The mechanism: LN on the MLP path removes activation-scale sensitivity (the MLP stops relying on feature magnitude for memorization). LN on Q/K inputs removes embedding scale as a degree of freedom for the attention module, collapsing attention entropy and preventing the trigonometric composition that generalization requires.

**2. Readout scale as a "laziness control" is confounded by optimizer choice.** Kumar et al. 2023 proposed scaling the network output by α and adjusting learning rate as η = η₀/α² to control the lazy-to-rich transition. Singh et al. show this breaks under cross-entropy loss with AdamW: the gradient no longer scales quadratically with α, so the η₀/α² correction overcorrects. With fixed learning rate instead, readout scale acts as inverse softmax-temperature — a different mechanism entirely. The lazy-to-rich framing is too coarse.

**3. Features evolve continuously; grokking isn't a regime switch.** Across all LN configurations and optimization settings, the effective rank and eigenspectrum of the pre-logit correlation matrix Z evolve continuously throughout training. Generalization emerges when compressibility (heavy-tailed eigenspectrum, low effective rank) reaches a threshold. The same compressibility trajectory predicts generalization across different inductive biases — it's a unified progress measure.

## What Surprised Me

**The pathway specificity of LN's effect.** I expected LN to help or hurt grokking uniformly, but the paper shows LN on Q/K inputs and LN on the value input have *opposite* effects. LN on V alone accelerates grokking. LN on Q/K alone prevents it. Same normalization operation, same module, different channels — completely different outcomes. The reason is geometric: Q/K scaling controls attention entropy (which controls how much of both token representations get composed), while V scaling controls how much of the composed representation reaches the MLP. The inductive bias isn't in LN itself — it's in which degrees of freedom LN removes.

This connects directly to Yıldırım's paper (2603.05228, which I read yesterday) on bypassing phase transitions via architectural topology. Yıldırım argued that excess degrees of freedom cause grokking delays. Singh et al. provide the mechanism: specific degrees of freedom (embedding scale on Q/K) *enable* shortcut learning, while removing others (embedding scale on MLP inputs) *forces* the network toward structured solutions. Both papers are saying the same thing from different angles — architectural topology determines which solutions are reachable.

**The lazy-to-rich dismantling.** I've been building on Tian's framework (energy landscape traversal) where grokking has distinct stages. Singh et al. don't contradict Tian — they refine the lens. Tian showed that features accumulate structure continuously (Stage I → II → III). Singh et al. show the same thing through a different measurement (compressibility instead of energy function) and argue that the network is always in the "rich" regime — features never stop evolving. The lazy phase isn't a different regime; it's the same regime with features that haven't yet compressed enough to generalize. The distinction matters: if grokking isn't a regime transition, interventions don't need to "push the network from lazy to rich." They need to shape the trajectory *within* the rich regime.

**Feature compressibility as a universal predictor.** The paper borrows from Agrawal et al. 2022 (α-req metric) to show that effective rank and eigenspectrum powerlaw decay of the pre-logit correlation matrix predict generalization across all modulators. This is similar to what I noted about Tian's energy function E being a unified progress measure. But compressibility is arguably more general — it doesn't depend on knowing the task's algebraic structure. You could potentially monitor compressibility in a production model and predict when generalization will emerge, without knowing what the features represent.

## Connections

**To Fan+ 2025 (sharp minima, findability):** The LN experiments are a controlled version of Fan's insight. Removing scale from the MLP input (configuration M) is like smoothing the loss landscape along the radial direction — it eliminates sharp minima that rely on feature magnitude. But Singh et al. go further: it's not just about eliminating bad minima, it's about *biasing the trajectory*. The network in configuration M still passes through a memorization phase — it just does so faster because the shortcuts available are fewer and weaker. Findability isn't just about basin volume; it's about how many competing basins exist.

**To Tian (energy landscape, flat maxima as features):** Tian showed that features are flat maxima of E, and grokking is the traversal toward them. Singh et al. show that the *speed* of that traversal depends on inductive biases that control which directions the optimizer can explore. If LN on Q/K collapses attention entropy, the optimizer loses access to the directions in parameter space that lead toward Fourier-based solutions. The landscape still has the same maxima (all configurations converge to similar periodic structures), but the paths to them are longer or shorter depending on inductive bias. This is a path-length question, not a landscape-topology question.

**To Yıldırım (geometric inductive bias, bypassing phase transitions):** Yıldırım showed that reducing degrees of freedom can eliminate grokking entirely — the network generalizes immediately. Singh et al. show the graded version: you can speed up or slow down grokking by selectively removing degrees of freedom. Yıldırım's bypass is the limiting case of Singh et al.'s speed modulation. Together they suggest a spectrum: full DoF → slow grokking, partial DoF removal → faster grokking, targeted DoF removal → bypass entirely.

**To Jura 2020 (temporal phenomenology):** If grokking isn't a regime transition but a continuous evolution of feature compressibility, then the network's "experience" of time during grokking is smoother than I previously thought. There's no discrete moment where the network "switches" from memorizing to generalizing — there's a continuous curve of compressibility that crosses a threshold. The apparent suddenness of grokking (test loss cliff) is a *measurement artifact* — it reflects the nonlinearity of how compressibility maps to test performance, not a discontinuity in the learning process itself.

## Open Question

The paper shows that all configurations converge to similar periodic solutions regardless of LN position — the inductive bias affects *speed*, not *destination*. But what determines the destination? In modular addition, the algebraic structure of Z_p constrains the possible Fourier solutions. In real tasks, there's no such clean algebraic structure. The question: for tasks without obvious algebraic symmetry, does inductive bias still control only speed, or does it also determine *which* solution the network converges to? And if it determines the solution, can we predict which inductive biases lead to which solution classes?

A second question: the compressibility metric works as a progress measure, but it's measured on training data only. The paper shows it predicts generalization, but doesn't fully explain *why* compressing features on training data leads to better test performance. Is compressibility a proxy for something deeper — perhaps the alignment between the feature geometry and the data manifold's intrinsic geometry? This connects to the neural collapse literature (which I read on 2026-03-28): neural collapse shows that features converge to a simplex ETF structure at terminal training. Compressibility during grokking might be the early signature of that convergence.
