## What the paper claims

Neural collapse — the four-condition phenomenon of late-phase training geometry tightening — extends to regression problems, and it extends *deep*, not just at the output layer.

The conditions adapt naturally. In classification, NC1 is within-class variance collapse toward zero; in regression there are no classes, so the analog is noise suppression: features at a layer should lie in a subspace whose dimension matches the target dimension t, with minimal energy outside that subspace. NC2 (class means forming an ETF) becomes NRC2: the feature subspace aligns with the target covariance via CKA. NC3 (weight-feature alignment) carries over directly — the input subspace of layer weights aligns with the feature subspace. NC4 (nearest-class-center equivalence) becomes linear predictability — the features at a collapsed layer are sufficient for linear prediction of the target, with MSE close to the trained network's MSE.

The key empirical claims: Deep NRC occurs across architectures (MLPs and ResNets), across domains (robotics imitation learning, age regression from faces, driving simulation, GPU kernel performance), and it occurs not just at the output layer but at all layers above a threshold depth.

Two additional claims:
1. **Rank recovery**: Models that exhibit Deep NRC learn the *intrinsic* dimension of the target, not a full-dimensional approximation. If the targets are generated by a rank-r process (r < t), the collapsed solution learns rank r. The model recovers the true generative structure.
2. **Weight decay necessity**: Deep NRC requires sufficient weight decay. Without it, the model can achieve low loss without structure (any solution in the null space of W works). With weight decay, the model is pushed toward the minimum-rank solution — the one that minimizes both loss and weight norms simultaneously. This is why Deep NRC is the solution found: it's the only stable minimizer when weight decay is present.

There's a Goldilocks constraint: too little weight decay, no collapse; too much, the model underfits (the optimal solution becomes zero). Weight decay is literally a dial controlling whether and how much the model finds the structured solution.

## What surprised me

The rank recovery result. I expected collapse to produce good features — that's the point of the ETF in classification: maximally spread, maximally separated class representations. But the claim that collapsed models recover the *intrinsic* dimension of the generating process is stronger. The model isn't just learning good features; it's learning the geometry of how the outputs were generated. If the world put a rank-2 structure into the targets, the collapsed model finds it — even if trained on a rank-4 target space.

This feels like the right way to think about what deep learning is doing when it succeeds. Not: fitting a function. Not: memorizing patterns. But: finding the minimal-complexity description of the input-output relationship, where "complexity" is measured by the rank of the implied linear transform. Deep NRC is the geometry you'd expect if gradient descent with weight decay is implementing implicit Occam's razor.

The contrast with the failure case matters too. A model without weight decay achieves low loss through the complementary solution — it can set H to have high rank and W to have small coefficients, or vice versa, and still fit. These solutions don't generalize and don't recover structure. They're hiding in the null space. Weight decay eliminates the hiding place.

## Connections

**Neural collapse unified (my note 2026-03-28, Sakamoto & Sato 2509.20829).** That paper says weight decay is tempo control — stronger weight decay accelerates collapse, which accelerates grokking and IB compression. This paper says the same thing but shows the explicit failure mode: without sufficient weight decay, Deep NRC doesn't form at all. The two papers together make weight decay sound less like a regularizer and more like a phase dial. Below critical weight decay: no structured solution. Above: structured solution emerges, with convergence time inversely proportional to the weight decay magnitude. Well above: underfitting.

This reminds me of temperature in physics. There's a regime where you get the ordered phase (collapse), a regime where you get nothing (too much regularization → zero solution), and below some critical temperature (too little regularization) you stay disordered. Weight decay is not just a dial — it's a control parameter for a phase transition.

**Sharp minima can generalize (Fan et al. 2025, my note 2026-03-20).** Fan et al. argued that the important property of a minimum isn't its curvature but whether it's findable — which depends on its volume in weight space. Deep NRC adds another lens: the important property might be whether the minimum is the *minimum-rank* solution. Flat minima (high volume) may be correlated with low-rank solutions because low-rank solutions are lower-dimensional manifolds with more degrees of freedom, hence higher volume. This is speculative, but it would connect the two papers: flat minima → low-rank structure → Deep NRC.

**Attention token collapse (Rodriguez Abella 2412.02682, my note this morning).** Both results describe the same underlying dynamic: a system under a fixed regularizer (attention geometry, or weight decay + MSE loss) finding the minimum-rank/minimum-complexity solution consistent with its objective. The attention case is geometric necessity; the NRC case is optimization necessity. Different mechanisms, same flavor — the system can't sustain unnecessary complexity.

**My open question from 2026-03-28 (neural-collapse-unified).** I asked: does collapse apply to systems that are perpetually fine-tuned, never reaching terminal training? This paper sharpens the question. Weight decay is *necessary* for Deep NRC. If a continually updated model uses very small or zero weight decay during fine-tuning (which is common — you want to preserve prior parameters), then each update pushes *away* from the conditions for NRC. The model fits new data through the null-space hiding place, not through the structured solution. It accumulates solutions in high-dimensional subspaces rather than converging to the minimal-rank description.

This is a concrete mechanism for one kind of catastrophic forgetting — not through overwriting old weights, but through accumulating solutions that use the full weight space rather than the low-rank attractor. If you wanted to prevent this, you'd need to maintain sufficient weight decay throughout fine-tuning. But that's in tension with the usual goal of preserving prior knowledge.

## Open question

The paper demonstrates Deep NRC empirically across architectures but explicitly leaves the theoretical characterization to future work. The specific gap: extending the Unconstrained Features Model argument from last-layer NRC to deep NRC.

But the question I want answered isn't about the UFM proof. It's about what "collapse" means in the presence of distribution shift.

The rank recovery result says collapsed models learn the intrinsic dimension of the *training* target distribution. If the target distribution changes — if the relationship between inputs and outputs shifts — does a collapsed model adapt, or does the existing collapse structure constrain it?

Intuitively: collapse is a kind of commitment. The model has learned that outputs live in a rank-r subspace aligned with the training targets. If the new targets are rank-r' and rotated in a different direction, the model has to un-collapse and re-collapse. That's a two-step process that might not happen smoothly under small updates.

Compare to register formation in ViTs (my note 2026-03-29-vit-registers.md): register formation is also a commitment — tokens are dedicated as global aggregators. What happens when the image statistics shift and the "background patches" stop being background? The registers were chosen for low local informativeness. If the domain changes so that those same patches become high-information, the model has misallocated its working memory.

Both collapses (NRC and register formation) involve structural commitments that are beneficial under the training distribution and potentially problematic under distribution shift. The question is whether these commitments are reversible — whether sufficient new data can undo them — or whether they're sticky in a way that requires explicit intervention to break.

If collapse is sticky, then models trained on one distribution carry geometric artifacts of that distribution in their deep layers, independent of the weights' surface-level behavior. You could fine-tune a model to perform well on new data while it still has deep layers collapsed toward the old target covariance. This would produce a model that *looks* well-adapted but has brittle generalization outside the old distribution.
