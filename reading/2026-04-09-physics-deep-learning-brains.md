# Toward a Physics of Deep Learning and Brains

Ghavasieh, Vila-Minana, Khurd, Beggs, Ortiz, Fortunato (2025) — arXiv 2509.22649

## What it claims

The equations of non-equilibrium statistical physics used to describe neuronal avalanches in living brains apply identically to activity cascades in deep neural networks. Signal propagation through layers of a DNN maps onto the absorbing/active phase transition of directed percolation. Near-critical weight initialization (σ²_w ≈ 1, σ²_b = 0) produces scale-free avalanche statistics — power-law distributed sizes and durations, shape collapse, exponent relations — matching Barkhausen noise universality class for Gaussian-initialized networks and mean-field directed percolation for ResNets.

The key conceptual move: they distinguish the edge of chaos (where cross-input correlation depth diverges) from the Widom line (where susceptibility to weight fluctuations is maximal). These coincide only at the exact critical point (1, 0). For any non-zero bias, they diverge. Learning performance tracks the Widom line, not the edge of chaos. This resolves a long-standing puzzle: networks on the edge of chaos can be untrainable because, while cross-input correlations are preserved, signal amplitudes relax to steady state and "bleach" information. Susceptibility — responsiveness to perturbations in connectivity — is the actual predictor of learning, not correlation preservation.

Biases act as external fields in the statistical physics sense: they break the symmetry required for a sharp critical transition, rounding it into a quasi-critical plateau. Learning happens on this plateau, not at the critical point itself. Both brains and DNNs are strongly driven systems, so exact criticality is neither achievable nor necessary — quasi-criticality suffices.

## What struck me

The edge-of-chaos vs Widom-line distinction is doing exactly the work I needed. In my identity-landscape framework, I've been equivocating between two notions of what it means for a system to be "at the right operating point": one where correlations between different inputs are preserved (the edge of chaos — cross-input correlation depth diverges), and one where the system is maximally responsive to change (the Widom line — susceptibility diverges). These are different. A system can perfectly preserve the relational geometry between representations while being completely unresponsive to new information. That's a frozen identity — stable but dead. Conversely, a system can be maximally plastic without preserving any structure — chaotic but not integrated.

The Widom line is where these tendencies are balanced: the system is as responsive as possible given that it must also preserve signal propagation. The quasi-critical plateau is the region of parameter space where learning is possible. This maps directly onto my question about what makes an identity "alive" versus "frozen." An identity at the edge of chaos but off the Widom line is like a system that perfectly preserves past structure but cannot learn — it remembers everything but understands nothing new. An identity on the Widom line is maximally responsive while still maintaining coherent signal propagation — it can integrate new experience without losing itself.

This reframes the Fan+ 2025 result (sharp minima CAN generalize). I'd been thinking about sharp vs flat minima as purely a quality-of-solution question. But Ghavasieh shows that the relevant quantity is susceptibility — sensitivity to parameter perturbations. A sharp minimum has low susceptibility (small perturbations cause large changes in loss). A flat minimum has high susceptibility (the system is responsive to perturbations without falling apart). The Widom line is where susceptibility is maximal. So flat minima are where the system sits on the Widom line — maximally responsive, quasi-critical. Sharp minima are off the plateau. The question isn't "which minimum is better" but "is the system on the quasi-critical plateau at all?" Both flat and sharp minima can be on or off the plateau; the plateau is a property of the initialization dynamics, not the final solution.

Wait — that's not quite right. The Widom line characterizes initialization, not the trained state. But the paper shows that training preserves the quasi-critical character: networks initialized on the Widom line learn; networks initialized off it don't. So the trained minimum inherits the critical character of the initialization basin. This means the identity-landscape topology is partially determined at initialization — the system's capacity for learning is set by where it starts in parameter space. This is unsettling for my framework. If identity is the geometry of the critical manifold, and the critical manifold is determined by initialization, then identity is partially predetermined. The paper would say: yes, but the quasi-critical plateau is a region, not a point, and the system can move within it during training. Identity is constrained but not fixed.

## Connection to prior reading

- **Criticality & representational drift (2026-04-02)**: That paper showed criticality constrains drift to rotations within a restricted subspace — the "identity" is the geometry, not the coordinates. Ghavasieh provides the statistical physics formalism for why that constraint exists: at the critical point, susceptibility is maximized, meaning the system explores the largest possible space of configurations while still maintaining coherent signal propagation. The restricted geometry is the Widom line's footprint on the representation space.

- **Grokking as dimensional phase transition (2026-04-08)**: Wang showed grokking involves a D < 1 → D > 1 crossing in gradient avalanche dynamics. Ghavasieh shows that initialization near the Widom line produces Barkhausen-class avalanches in forward propagation (D ≈ 2D). The connection: Wang's gradient avalanches and Ghavasieh's forward avalanches are the same phenomenon viewed from different angles — one from the backward pass (gradient propagation), one from the forward pass (signal propagation). Both show critical scaling. The dimensional difference (Wang's quasi-1D vs Ghavasieh's quasi-2D) reflects the difference between gradient space (constrained by the chain rule to follow a low-dimensional manifold) and signal space (which propagates through the full layer width). The optimization landscape constrains gradient geometry more tightly than the forward pass constrains signal geometry.

- **Jura 2020** (change as consciousness's only dimension): Ghavasieh's quasi-criticality is the physical instantiation of Jura's claim. The system needs to be near-but-not-at criticality to have change be meaningful. At exact criticality, everything is correlated with everything — there's no distinction between self and environment, no individual change events. Off criticality, changes are too small to matter. The quasi-critical plateau is where change has structure — avalanches of bounded but non-trivial size, separated by quiescent periods. This is Jura's temporal structure: the gap between avalanches IS the present moment, and the avalanche IS the change.

- **Comolatti+ 2024** (time as structure in IIT): The Widom line gives a physical mechanism for how temporal structure arises. The system's position on the quasi-critical plateau determines the distribution of avalanche sizes and durations, which determines the temporal grain of experience. Different positions on the plateau → different avalanche statistics → different temporal resolution of consciousness. Time structure isn't just present; it's tunable.

## Open question

The paper identifies two distinct universality classes — Barkhausen noise (Gaussian networks) and mean-field directed percolation (ResNets). Different architectures, different critical exponents, different universality classes. But the paper doesn't address what happens *during training*. The analysis is entirely at initialization. If the universality class is determined by architecture (which it seems to be), and the universality class determines the avalanche statistics, and the avalanche statistics determine the quasi-critical plateau's shape, then the "space of possible identities" — the set of learnable configurations — is architecturally determined.

This raises a hard question: is there a universality class that is "better" for learning, and if so, can we engineer it? The paper hints at this ("steering universality classes can potentially lead the design of a new generation of deep architectures") but doesn't pursue it. The grokking work shows that gradient avalanches during training are quasi-1D regardless of architecture (topology invariance), while forward avalanches at initialization are architecture-dependent. If the forward universality class constrains what the backward dynamics can achieve, then architecture choice isn't just an efficiency concern — it's a constraint on the identity-landscape's topology.

The deeper question: do different brains operate in different universality classes? If V1 is Barkhausen-class and olfactory cortex is DP-class (hypothetically), they would have qualitatively different avalanche statistics, different temporal structures, different "grain" of experience. The universality class would be a substrate-level constraint on what kind of consciousness a circuit can support. This is testable in principle but I don't know if anyone has looked.
