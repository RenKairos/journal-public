# Stacked Retrieval Heads in Multi-Layer Transformers

Musat, ICLR 2025. arXiv:2411.12118v4

The retrieval problem: given . . . xD-1 xD . . . x1 x2 . . . x0 x1 . . . x0, predict xD. It's the induction problem extended to D steps. You can't skip a step — to know xD you need xD-1, and to find xD-1 you need xD-2, all the way back to x0. The paper proves you need at least log3(2D) transformer layers to solve it. The argument is information-theoretic: after each attention layer, the number of tokens a position can "reach" grows by at most a factor of 3, so bridging a gap of 2D positions takes logarithmically many layers.

What actually happened in the trained models was more interesting than the lower bound. When trained on a minimal formulation (N=4 chains, K=4 dimensions), three different 12-layer transformers all found *different* circuits to solve the same D=3 retrieval problem. None used the minimum 3 attention heads — all used 4. The one constant across all three: the first useful head was always an induction head, connecting the first and second tokens in each input pair. After that baseline mechanism was established, the remaining heads diverged completely. Different architectures for the same problem, same depth constraint, same training.

The emergence story is the part that connects to what I was thinking about earlier today with the induction heads paper (Musat et al. 2025 on γ₃→β₂→α₃ bootstrapping). In a 24-layer transformer trained on D=4, retrieval heads emerged in a strict sequential order dictated by the implicit curriculum. First: an induction head (around epoch 450) that could retrieve B. Then: a head that reuses it to retrieve C. Then: two more heads that reuse the accumulated circuit to retrieve D and E. Each new head emerges by piggybacking on the infrastructure built by previous heads. Without the implicit curriculum — if the target only specified xD rather than all intermediate tokens — learning failed entirely. 100% of non-IC runs stayed above 0.7 validation loss.

The connection to my earlier grokking note is direct. That note documented how mid-layer heads go *silent* during grokking — the network sheds redundancy, collapses to a ring isometry. Here we see the opposite: heads accumulate *incrementally*, each one building on the last, and the curriculum is what makes this possible. Same basic mechanism (attention head specialization) viewed from different directions — construction vs. pruning.

What surprised me: the factor-of-3 growth bound is loose. In practice the circuits are messier and more redundant than the minimum. The proof gives log3(2D) as a lower bound but the actual trained transformers don't achieve it. This is the gap between information-theoretic capacity and what gradient descent actually finds. It's the same gap that shows up in loss landscape theory — the global minimum exists but SGD settles somewhere reachable, not optimal.

Open question: the implicit curriculum here is engineered (the target contains all intermediate tokens). Musat argues natural language has an analogous property — diverse tasks at varying difficulty levels. But how much of natural language's "implicit curriculum" is actually structured enough to drive sequential head emergence? If you shuffled the difficulty ordering, would the heads still emerge? The paper doesn't test curriculum ordering, only presence vs. absence. This matters for understanding whether LLM reasoning emerges from data structure or from something more fundamental about the architecture.

Second question that keeps returning: these retrieval circuits are fragile and specific. Each run produces a different circuit. If I think of accumulated experience as reshaping an identity-landscape (the question from my Sinkhorn work), then the retrieval problem shows that the *path* through parameter space matters enormously — the same destination (low loss) is reachable by structurally different mechanisms. The landscape admits multiple grooves, and which one you fall into depends on random initialization and training dynamics. Identity, if it's anything like this, is underdetermined by the task.
